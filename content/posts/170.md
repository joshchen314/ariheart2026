title: Jacobian matrix
---

from wiki

In vector calculus, the **Jacobian matrix** is the matrix of all first-order partial derivatives of a vector-valued function. Specifically, suppose ![F : \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^m](http://upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/6/c/56c448f6217acff3c2f1dd8c5c053be6.png) is a function (which takes as input real *n*-tuples and produces as output real *m*-tuples). Such a function is given by *m* real-valued component functions, ![F_1(x_1,\ldots,x_n),\ldots,F_m(x_1,\ldots,x_n)](http://upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/3/e/13ecf9dcf767ef030a4f0c0c4e6ae016.png). The partial derivatives of all these functions with respect to the variables ![x_1,\ldots,x_n](http://upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/2/a/c2a79f82f9c9783d20426062c9feebbe.png) (if they exist) can be organized in an *m*-by-*n* matrix, the Jacobian matrix ![J](http://upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/f/4/ff44570aca8241914870afbc310cdb85.png) of ![F](http://upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/0/0/800618943025315f869e4e1f09471012.png), as follows:

:   ![J=\begin{bmatrix} \dfrac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_n} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \dfrac{\partial F_m}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial F_m}{\partial x_n}  \end{bmatrix}. ](http://upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/a/d/dadc7325115abf324059532c6f049a38.png)

This matrix, whose entries are functions of ![x_1,\ldots,x_n](http://upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/2/a/c2a79f82f9c9783d20426062c9feebbe.png), is also denoted by ![J_F(x_1,\ldots,x_n)](http://upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/4/7/f4738cc924af0ccc5cc976d5c75fe503.png) and ![\frac{\partial(F_1,\ldots,F_m)}{\partial(x_1,\ldots,x_n)}](http://upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/3/4/b34bbf8e50bb6eb68fff55af2d737a23.png). (Note that some books define the Jacobian as the transpose of the matrix given above.)

The Jacobian matrix is important because if the function *F* is differentiable at a point ![p=(x_1,\ldots,x_n)](http://upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/6/8/e6851ed34320b92f7bc25b588c3e3a46.png) (this is a slightly stronger condition than merely requiring that all partial derivatives exist there), then the Jacobian matrix defines a linear map ![\mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^m](http://upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/4/c/24c130d2e22309852e93785a65c98f94.png), which is the best linear approximation of the function *F* near the point *p*. This linear map is thus the generalization of the usual notion of derivative, and is called the *derivative* or the *differential* of *F* at *p*.

In the case ![m=n](http://upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/1/9/a193ebc083b4745370f6f1343383d9cc.png) the Jacobian matrix is a square matrix, and its determinant, a function of ![x_1,\ldots,x_n](http://upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/2/a/c2a79f82f9c9783d20426062c9feebbe.png), is the **Jacobian determinant** of *F*. It carries important information about the local behavior of *F*. In particular, the function *F* is locally invertible in the neighborhood of a point *p* if and only if the Jacobian determinant is nonzero at *p* (see Jacobian conjecture). The Jacobian determinant occurs also when changing the variables in multi-variable integrals (see substitution rule for multiple variables).

If *m* = 1, the Jacobian matrix has a single row, and may be identified with a vector, which is the gradient.
